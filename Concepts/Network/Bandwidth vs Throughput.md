### 1- Bandwidth

- **Definition**: The maximum capacity of a network link or channel to carry data, typically measured in bits per second (bps), such as Mbps (megabits per second) or Gbps (gigabits per second).
- Analogy: Think of it as the width of a pipe—how much data could theoretically flow through it at once.
- **Key Points**:
- Represents the theoretical maximum, not actual performance.
- Determined by the physical medium (e.g., fiber, copper) and network hardware.
- Example: A 1 Gbps Ethernet connection has a bandwidth of 1 gigabit per second.
- - **Context**: Often advertised by ISPs as the potential speed of your connection.

### 2- Throughput

- **Definition**: The actual amount of data successfully transmitted over a network in a given time, also measured in bps (e.g., Mbps, Gbps).
- **Analogy**: The amount of water actually flowing through the pipe, which may be less than the pipe’s full capacity due to clogs or leaks.
- **Key Points**:

- Reflects real-world performance, affected by factors like network congestion, packet loss, latency, and protocol overhead.
- Usually lower than bandwidth due to inefficiencies or bottlenecks.
- Example: A 1 Gbps link might have a throughput of 800 Mbps due to delays or interference.
- **Context**: Used to measure effective network performance in practice.
